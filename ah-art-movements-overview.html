Arts & Humanities – Art Movements Overview

High-yield movements with key traits and a few flagship artists to recognize immediately.

Link to artists & works Compare & contrast

Impressionism

Late 1800s, mostly France
  • Light, loose brushstrokes; capturing a "moment in time."
  • Interest in changing light and color; often painted outdoors (en plein air).
  • Key names: Claude Monet, Pierre-Auguste Renoir, Edgar Degas, Berthe Morisot, Mary Cassatt.

Post-Impressionism

Late 1800s into early 1900s
  • Builds on Impressionism but pushes color, form, or emotion further.
  • Cézanne – structure and form; Mont Sainte-Victoire, The Card Players.
  • Van Gogh – expressive brushwork and intense color; Starry Night, Sunflowers.
  • Gauguin – flat areas of pure color and symbolism; Vision After the Sermon.

Expressionism

Early 1900s, especially Germany & Northern Europe
  • Distorted forms and bold colors to show inner feelings, not realistic appearances.
  • Includes groups like Die Brücke and Der Blaue Reiter.
  • Key names: Edvard Munch (The Scream), Kandinsky, Franz Marc.

Cubism

Early 1900s, Picasso & Braque
  • Objects broken into geometric shapes and shown from multiple viewpoints.
  • Analytic Cubism – muted colors, very fractured forms.
  • Synthetic Cubism – collage, non-art materials like newspaper.

Surrealism

1920s onward
  • Influenced by Freud and dreams; strange, dreamlike combinations of objects.
  • Key names: Salvador Dalí (The Persistence of Memory), René Magritte, Max Ernst, Joan Miró.
